🔷 16.1 Mathematical Foundation 

16.1.1 Domain

\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d, \quad \Omega \text{ is compact}

i.e., \Omega is closed and bounded, containing all admissible system states.

\Psi \in \Omega \;\Rightarrow\; \|\Psi\| \le M

for some finite constant M > 0.

⸻

16.1.2 State Variable

\Psi_k \in \Omega

The state variable is assumed to be:
	•	measurable
	•	bounded

⸻

16.1.3 Potential Function

V \in C^2(\Omega), \quad \nabla^2 V(x) \succeq m I, \; m > 0

Thus, V(x) is strongly convex, implying:
	•	existence of a unique global minimum
	•	absence of multiple equilibria

⸻

16.1.4 Energy Functional

\mathcal{E}(\Psi) = V(\Psi) + \frac{1}{2}\|\nabla \Psi\|^2

which is a convex functional.

⸻

16.1.5 Probability Space

P(x,t) \ge \varepsilon > 0, \quad \int_\Omega P(x,t)\,dx = 1

ensuring:
	•	absence of singularities
	•	well-defined logarithmic terms \ln P

⸻

16.1.6 Lipschitz Continuity

\|F(x) - F(y)\| \le L \|x - y\|

for some L > 0, ensuring regularity of the system dynamics.

⸻

16.1.7 Existence and Uniqueness

Under the above assumptions, the system admits a unique solution trajectory:

by the Picard–Lindelöf theorem.

⸻

16.1.8 Time Discretization

t_k = k\,dt, \quad dt < \frac{1}{L}

ensuring numerical stability of the discrete-time approximation.

⸻

 Closure Statement (Part I)

\boxed{
\text{The system is well-posed, bounded, and admits a unique solution.}
}
